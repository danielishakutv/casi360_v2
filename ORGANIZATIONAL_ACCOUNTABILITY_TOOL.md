## 1. Thematic Area and Subtheme Classification

### Primary Thematic Area

**Accountability and Transparency**

CASI360 is a digital accountability infrastructure project. Its core function is to make organizational operations — financial transactions, procurement decisions, HR actions, and project management — fully transparent, traceable, and auditable by design. Every feature of the platform exists to ensure that organizations cannot operate opaquely, even unintentionally.

### Secondary Thematic Areas

**Policy and Advocacy**
CASI360 provides the evidence base that civil society organizations need to credibly advocate for accountability standards. Organizations that use the platform can demonstrate — with verifiable data — what good governance looks like in practice, strengthening their voice in policy discussions around public financial management, institutional reform, and CSO regulation.

**Social Inclusion**
The platform's role-based access and permission management system ensures that organizational processes are governed by defined rules rather than individual discretion. This reduces the potential for gatekeeping, ensures equitable access to information within organizations, and creates documented processes that protect all staff — regardless of position, gender, or status — from arbitrary decision-making.

### Subthemes

**Primary Subtheme: Open Government Partnership Engagement**

CASI360 directly supports the principles of the Open Government Partnership (OGP) at the organizational level. The OGP framework calls for transparency, citizen participation, accountability, and technology & innovation. CASI360 operationalizes these principles within civil society institutions by:

- Making all organizational transactions and decisions transparent through permanent audit trails
- Enabling participation through structured approval workflows where multiple stakeholders review and authorize actions
- Holding individuals accountable through role-based access controls, documented approvals, and tamper-proof records
- Leveraging technology to replace informal, opaque processes with structured, evidence-generating systems

When CSOs demand open governance from public institutions, they must first demonstrate it within their own operations. CASI360 provides the infrastructure to do so credibly.

**Secondary Subtheme: Leadership Development**

Organizational leaders — executive directors, finance managers, programme heads — require systems that support good governance rather than depending entirely on individual integrity. CASI360 develops leadership capacity by providing structured decision-making frameworks (multi-level approvals, configurable thresholds, documented rejections), giving emerging leaders clear operational boundaries and evidence-based accountability practices that they carry forward throughout their careers.

**Additional Subtheme: Policy Analysis & Evidence-Informed Advocacy**

The platform's comprehensive reporting and audit capabilities generate real operational data that organizations can use to analyze their own governance performance, identify systemic weaknesses, and build evidence-informed cases for internal policy reform and external advocacy on accountability standards.

## 2. Project Title

**CASI360: A Digital Accountability Platform for Strengthening Transparency and Institutional Integrity in Civil Society Organizations**

---

## 3. Executive Summary

CASI360 is a purpose-built digital management platform that embeds transparency and accountability into the daily operations of civil society organizations (CSOs), non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and mission-driven institutions. Rather than treating accountability as an afterthought or a reporting burden, CASI360 makes it the natural outcome of how the organization works — every transaction recorded, every approval traceable, every decision documented.

In contexts where public trust in institutions is fragile and donor confidence depends on demonstrable stewardship, CASI360 provides the operational backbone that ensures no action goes unrecorded, no expenditure bypasses oversight, and no individual operates beyond their authorized scope.

---

## 4. Problem Statement

Organizations working in the development, humanitarian, and social impact sectors face persistent accountability challenges:

- **Financial opacity** — Procurement decisions and payments are made without structured oversight, creating room for misuse of funds and undermining donor confidence.
- **Undocumented decision-making** — Approvals happen informally through phone calls, verbal instructions, or unrecorded internal agreements, leaving no evidence trail for audits or institutional memory.
- **Weak internal controls** — Staff access sensitive data or authorize transactions beyond their role, with no system to enforce boundaries or detect irregularities.
- **Fragmented records** — Employee data, procurement files, project documentation, and communication records are scattered across spreadsheets, email inboxes, paper files, and personal devices — making audits painful and compliance unreliable.
- **Difficulty proving accountability** — Even well-governed organizations struggle to demonstrate their accountability practices to donors, regulators, and the communities they serve, because the evidence is locked in informal systems.

These challenges are not unique to poorly managed organizations. They are systemic — the result of operating without infrastructure designed for accountability.

### The Broader Context

In Nigeria and across sub-Saharan Africa, civil society organizations play a critical role in service delivery, rights advocacy, peacebuilding, and democratic governance. Yet the sector's credibility is frequently undermined by high-profile cases of financial mismanagement, weak internal controls, and the inability of even well-intentioned organizations to produce verifiable evidence of their stewardship. This erodes public trust, discourages donor investment, and weakens the position of CSOs when they advocate for transparency from government and the private sector.

The accountability gap is not primarily a question of will. Many organizations want to be transparent but lack the tools and systems to make transparency operational. Paper-based processes, manual spreadsheets, and informal approval chains cannot produce the kind of auditable, tamper-proof evidence that modern accountability standards demand.

## 5. Proposed Solution: CASI360

CASI360 is a web-based platform that centralizes an organization's core operational functions — human resources, procurement, project management, financial tracking, and internal communication — under a single system governed by three foundational principles:

### 1. Every Action is Recorded

CASI360 maintains a permanent, unalterable audit trail of every action taken in the system. When an employee is hired, a purchase order is created, a payment is recorded, or a document is approved — the system captures who did it, what was done, when it happened, and from where. This record cannot be edited or deleted by any user, including administrators.

This means the organization always has a verifiable account of its operations, available on demand for internal review, external audit, or donor reporting.

### 2. No Transaction Bypasses Oversight

Financial transactions in CASI360 follow a structured, multi-level approval workflow. A purchase request or order cannot proceed to payment without passing through a defined chain of reviewers — from managerial review, to financial verification, to operations clearance, to executive sign-off (for high-value transactions). The number of approval levels scales automatically with the value of the transaction.

Key safeguards include:

- **Sequential approval** — Each reviewer must complete their step before the next can act. No step can be skipped.
- **Self-approval prevention** — By default, the person who submits a request cannot approve it, eliminating a common avenue for abuse.
- **Rejection with documentation** — If a transaction is rejected at any stage, the reviewer must provide a written reason. The submitter can then revise and resubmit, starting a fresh approval cycle.
- **Configurable thresholds** — The organization sets its own financial thresholds for escalation, ensuring oversight is proportional to risk.

### 3. Access is Governed by Role and Permission

CASI360 enforces role-based access control across the entire platform. Every user is assigned a role (Administrator, Manager, or Staff), and each role carries a specific set of permissions that determine what information the user can view, create, edit, or act upon. A granular permission matrix — managed by authorized administrators — allows the organization to tailor access to its structure and policies.

This means:

- A finance officer can record payments but cannot alter employee records.
- A project coordinator can view project budgets but cannot approve procurement.
- A staff member can submit a purchase request but cannot approve their own or anyone else's request without explicit authorization.

The system enforces these boundaries automatically. Users only see the features and data relevant to their role — reducing exposure to sensitive information and ensuring operational boundaries are maintained.

## 6. Core Features and Their Accountability Value

### Human Resources Management

| What It Does | Why It Matters for Accountability |
|---|---|
| Maintains a complete digital record of every employee — their role, department, reporting line, employment dates, and status | Ensures the organization can always verify who was authorized to act and in what capacity at any point in time |
| Tracks employee status changes (active, on leave, terminated) with dates and responsible parties | Prevents "ghost workers" and provides auditable employment history |
| Organizes employees into departments and designations with clear hierarchies | Establishes the organizational structure that governs responsibility and oversight |
| Supports structured performance, disciplinary, and commendation notes tied to individual employees | Creates a documented record of HR actions, reducing the risk of arbitrary or undocumented decisions |

### Procurement and Financial Oversight

| What It Does | Why It Matters for Accountability |
|---|---|
| Manages the full procurement lifecycle: need identification, requisition, approval, purchase ordering, goods receipt, and payment | Ensures every procurement action is traceable from origin to completion, with no gaps in the chain |
| Enforces multi-level approval workflows that scale with transaction value | Prevents unauthorized spending and ensures proportional oversight for high-value transactions |
| Tracks vendor records including banking details, transaction history, and status | Maintains a verifiable supplier registry, reducing the risk of fraudulent or undisclosed vendor relationships |
| Monitors inventory levels with reorder alerts | Prevents over-procurement and provides evidence of stock management |
| Records every payment (disbursement) against approved orders, with method, reference, date, and responsible officer | Creates a complete payment trail tied to authorized procurement, ensuring every outflow is justified and documented |
| Automatically prevents payments from exceeding approved amounts | Eliminates overpayment and enforces financial discipline at the system level |
| Tracks payment status (unpaid, partially paid, fully paid) for every purchase order | Provides real-time visibility into outstanding financial obligations |

### Project Management and Donor Stewardship

| What It Does | Why It Matters for Accountability |
|---|---|
| Tracks projects with detailed budgets broken down by standard cost categories (Personnel, Travel, Equipment, Training, M&E, etc.) | Enables the organization to demonstrate precisely how project funds are allocated and utilized |
| Records donor contributions per project with donor type, contact details, and amount | Provides a clear record of funding sources and commitments for donor reporting and compliance |
| Tracks implementing, technical, funding, and logistics partners per project | Documents the partnership ecosystem for each initiative, supporting transparency in collaboration |
| Assigns team members to projects with defined roles and time periods | Creates accountability for who is responsible for what within each project |
| Monitors activities and milestones with status tracking and completion percentages | Enables evidence-based progress reporting to donors, boards, and stakeholders |
| Supports project notes with external document links for supplementary evidence | Builds a comprehensive, living project record accessible to authorized stakeholders |

### Internal Communication and Institutional Transparency

| What It Does | Why It Matters for Accountability |
|---|---|
| Provides direct messaging between staff with threaded conversations | Replaces informal, undocumented communication with a system that preserves institutional knowledge |
| Hosts department-level and organization-wide discussion forums | Creates shared spaces for transparent dialogue, reducing information silos |
| Supports official notices with audience targeting (all staff, specific departments, specific roles) | Ensures organizational directives reach the intended audience with proof of distribution |
| Tracks notice read receipts with timestamps and read percentages | Provides evidence that staff were informed of policies, decisions, and directives — critical for compliance |
| Supports priority levels (Normal, Important, Critical) and notice scheduling | Enables structured communication governance appropriate to the urgency and scope of information |

### Reporting and Evidence Generation

| What It Does | Why It Matters for Accountability |
|---|---|
| Generates reports across all modules: HR, Procurement, Projects, Finance, Communication, and Audit | Provides on-demand evidence for internal review, board reporting, and external audits |
| Supports export in CSV, Excel, and PDF formats | Enables easy sharing with donors, regulators, and oversight bodies in their preferred format |
| Includes comprehensive financial reports showing spending by department, total order values, disbursement totals, and outstanding balances | Delivers the financial transparency that donors and grant-makers require |
| Provides a dedicated audit log report showing all system actions, filterable by user, action type, or date | Gives auditors direct access to a complete, unalterable record of organizational activity |
| Logs all report downloads in the audit trail | Even the act of accessing data is transparent and traceable |

### System Security and Data Protection

| What It Does | Why It Matters for Accountability |
|---|---|
| Enforces strong password requirements and mandatory password changes for new users | Protects against unauthorized access to organizational data |
| Limits login attempts and records all access events with timestamps and device information | Detects and deters unauthorized access attempts |
| Preserves data integrity through "soft delete" — terminated employees, cancelled orders, and deactivated vendors remain in the system as historical records | Ensures data is never lost or destroyed, maintaining a complete institutional record |
| Protects against common security threats (cross-site attacks, injection attacks, session hijacking) | Safeguards the integrity of the accountability infrastructure itself |

## 7. Alignment with Transparency and Accountability Principles

CASI360 directly supports the core principles promoted by transparency and accountability frameworks in the development sector:

### Financial Transparency
Every financial transaction — from requisition to payment — flows through a documented, auditable process. The organization can demonstrate, at any time, what was spent, why, through whose authorization, and to whom.

### Decision Traceability
The multi-level approval workflow ensures that no financial or operational decision is made unilaterally. Every approval, rejection, and revision is recorded with the reviewer's identity, timestamp, and rationale.

### Information Accessibility
Role-based reporting enables different stakeholders — management, finance, project teams, board members — to access the information relevant to their oversight function without compromising data boundaries.

### Institutional Memory
The permanent audit trail and soft-delete architecture ensure that the organization's operational history is preserved regardless of staff turnover, leadership changes, or organizational restructuring.

### Conflict of Interest Prevention
Built-in self-approval restrictions and segregation of duties through the permission system reduce the risk of individuals having unchecked authority over financial decisions.

### Stakeholder Accountability
The notice system with read tracking, combined with comprehensive reporting capabilities, enables the organization to demonstrate accountability not only to donors but to staff, partners, and the communities it serves.

## 8. Project Objectives

### Overall Objective

To strengthen transparency, accountability, and institutional integrity within civil society organizations by providing a digital operational platform that makes auditable governance the default outcome of daily work — not an additional compliance exercise.

### Specific Objectives

1. **Deploy a functional digital accountability platform** that centralizes human resources, procurement, project management, financial tracking, and communication for CSOs, with built-in audit trails and multi-level approval workflows.

2. **Institutionalize financial oversight mechanisms** by replacing informal, paper-based procurement and payment processes with structured, documented workflows that require multi-level authorization and prevent self-approval.

3. **Build organizational governance capacity** by equipping CSO leadership, finance teams, and programme staff with a system that provides clear operational boundaries, documented decision-making, and real-time visibility into institutional operations.

4. **Strengthen donor accountability and reporting** by enabling organizations to generate comprehensive, verifiable financial and programmatic reports on demand, in formats suitable for donor compliance, external audits, and stakeholder communication.

5. **Promote Open Government principles within the CSO sector** by demonstrating that civil society organizations can hold themselves to the same transparency standards they advocate for public institutions — using technology to make accountability visible, measurable, and verifiable.

## 10. Project Activities

### Phase 1: Platform Development and Refinement
- Finalize and harden the CASI360 platform for deployment across multiple organizations
- Conduct security audits and data protection reviews
- Develop onboarding materials, user guides, and training curricula

### Phase 2: Pilot Deployment
- Deploy CASI360 to a cohort of partner CSOs (recommended: 5-10 organizations of varying sizes)
- Provide hands-on onboarding and training for administrators, managers, and staff
- Establish support channels for technical assistance during the adoption period

### Phase 3: Training and Capacity Building
- Conduct workshops on organizational accountability, financial transparency, and governance best practices — using CASI360 as the practical tool
- Train organizational leaders on interpreting audit logs, financial reports, and approval workflows
- Develop internal accountability policies aligned with the platform's capabilities

### Phase 4: Monitoring, Evaluation, and Documentation
- Track adoption metrics: number of users, transactions processed, approval workflows completed, reports generated
- Document governance improvements: reduction in informal approvals, increase in documented decisions, audit readiness improvements
- Collect case studies and testimonials from participating organizations

### Phase 5: Advocacy and Knowledge Sharing
- Publish findings and lessons learned on strengthening CSO accountability through digital infrastructure
- Present results to sector networks, donor roundtables, and governance forums
- Advocate for the adoption of digital accountability standards within the CSO sector

By investing in CASI360, a grant-maker invests in the institutional backbone of accountable civil society — not a one-time intervention, but a lasting infrastructure that strengthens every organization that adopts it and every community those organizations serve.
